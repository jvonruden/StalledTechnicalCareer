A Stalled Technical Career

There are two kinds of IT professionals, the kind who goes to work and when the day is done they go home and the only time they learn new skills is when their employee provides the training and they wait for the company to manage their career for them. After 10 years in their career, they think that they have 10 years of experience however in realty they just have 1 year of experience repeated 10 times and they are stuck re-living the past and they may have not realized it and their career has stalled out until are layed-off they start failing the technical interview questions during their search for a new job.

  • Pros:
  • There is nothing wrong with this approach to your career.
  • It is a great option if you priorities are a focused on family.
  • One Purpose for a job is to fulfill your addition to food, clothing, and shelter.
  • Avoiding change feels good and avoids fear, uncertainty, and doubt.
  • The longer you are at a company the more you will have goodwill and are valued on your subject matter knowledge.
  • Cons:
  • It takes a long time to discover the Cons and it took me 10 years to figure this out.
  • Wage Suppression aka company economics 101 equals inflationary raises and bonuses over the long term.
  • This means new hires for the same role as you will probably be paid higher than you.
  • Companies have to pay the going rate at that time for your role to attract new IT Professionals
  • This is where your avoiding fear, uncertainty, and doubt helps your employer.
  • Building goodwill and your subject matter knowledge will expire like a warehouse of bananas and hopefully you have maintained your tech skills.

The Other Kind of IT Professional

  • Are active in their community
  • Always learning new skills
  • Are good communicators
  • Have effective career behaviors

The other kind of IT professional is the ones who realize they need to manage their own career and take steps to build relationships, learn new skills, invest in their career, and are constantly making technology course corrections in their career that take them to the next level and then reap the rewards of all their hard work.

A Solution To Stalled Technical Career

Focusing on Community

I Want You to Be

  • Useful to your professional communities
  • Useful to communities you live in
  • Useful to others
  • Useful to your Employers
  • There are many solutions to a stalled technical career.
  • As a community leader, I am going to encourage you to focus on community as an approach to solve this problem.
  • Helping others is a reward all by its self.
  • Karma when you help other you will benefit too
  • Getting to know people in your community helps you during career and job searches.
  • When you need help you have large network of people to lean on for help.
  • Giving back to your community and help others learn new skills
  • Mentor or Coach others
  • Volunteer teach in your local schools, for example: teals12k.org
  • Learn new skills, have a balance between the leading edge and a diversity of knowledge.
  • Read books like Pragmatic Programmer, pluralsight, blogs to name a few
  • We forget when we learned new skills, our memory plays tricks on us and we think we always knew that skill. Our current memory of the skill is retroactively applied to our past memories of that skill.

High D’s - Dominance

Achieves success by taking decisive actions towards their goal

  • Task Focused
  • Assertive
  • Strengths: comfortable in a leadership role, problem solver, can make tough decisions, not afraid to confront issues, sees change as a challenge, stays focused on the mission.
  • Limitations (Others may see): intimidating, insensitive, brusque, impatient, intolerant, unapproachable
  • Examples: Vince Lombardi, Norman Schwarzkopf, Jane Fonda, Margaret Thatcher, George Patton
  • When communicating with them: Want others to be direct, straightforward, and communicate bottom line up front
  • Ready Fire Aim

High D’s

How They Talk

  • Ask What Questions
  • Tells vs. Asks
  • Talks More Than Listens
  • Go Right to The Issue
  • May Be Pushy, Even Rude
  • Fast Speech
  • Authoritative Tone of Control
  • Use Acronyms, Short Sentences

High D’s

What They Do

  • Task Focus, Results Oriented
  • Impatient
  • Direct, Forceful
  • Willing to Get in Trouble
  • Time Conscious
  • Good Eye Contact
  • History of Achievement
  • Can Rely on Gut Feelings

High D’s

Be Ready For

  • Blunt/demanding approach
  • Lack of empathy
  • Lack of sensitivity
  • Little social interaction

High D’s

You Should Try To

  • Communicate briefly/to the point
  • Respect their need for autonomy
  • Be clear about rules/expectations
  • Let them take the lead
  • Show your competence
  • Stick to the topic
  • Show independence

High I’s - Influence

Achieves success by persuading others to work with them towards the goal

  • People Focused
  • Assertive
  • Strengths:always available for others, inspiring to be around, spreads positive attitude, praises others.
  • Limitations (Others may see): tasks fall through the cracks, projects don’t get finished, lack organization
  • Examples: Willard Scott, Muhammad Ali, Tracy Ullman, Magic Johnson, Bill Clinton, Katie Couric
  • When communicating with them: Want others to be friendly, emotionally honest, and recognize the I’s contributions
  • Marketing People

High I’s

How They Talk

  • Ask who questions
  • Tell vs. ask
  • Make small talk
  • Go off on tangents
  • Use stories or anecdotes
  • Faster speech
  • Express their feelings
  • Share personal emotions

High I’s

What They Do

  • Animated
  • Lots of facial expression
  • Spontaneous
  • Laugh out loud
  • Stylish dress
  • Shorter attention span
  • Warm
  • May approach you closely

High I’s

Be Ready For

  • Attempts to persuade/influence
  • Need for the spotlight
  • Over-estimates self/others
  • Over-selling ideas
  • Vulnerable to feeling rejected

High I’s

You Should Try To

  • Approach them informally
  • Be relaxed and sociable
  • Let them tell you how they feel
  • Keep the conversation light
  • Provide written details
  • Give public recognition
  • Use humor

High S’s – Steadiness

Works with others as part of a team to achieve success

  • People Focused
  • Reserved
  • Strengths:good team player, empathetic to other's needs, methodical, good listener, easy to get along with, appreciative of others, praises
  • Limitations (Others may see):indecisive, indirect, resistant to change, sometimes worn down by other's problems
  • Examples: Mother Teresa, Mr. Rogers, Pope John Paul, John Denver, Charlie Gibson
  • When communicating with them: Want others to be to minimize socializing, and give details; they value accuracy and attention to detail.
  • Do not like to be publicly recognized

High S’s

How They Talk

  • Make small talk
  • Ask how questions
  • Ask vs. Tell
  • Listen more than talk
  • Slow, steady delivery
  • Reserved w/ opinions
  • Lower volume
  • Warmth in voice

High S’s

What They Do

  • Photos of relationships out

  • Casual relaxed walk
  • Patient, tolerant
  • Service oriented

High S’s

Be Ready For

High S’s

You Should Try To

High C’s Conscientious

Works within rules and procedures to ensure success

  • Task Focused
  • Reserved
  • Strengths: thorough, follows standards accurately, conscientious, diplomatic, accurate, fair (because following the rules does that).
  • Limitations (Others may see):overly concerned with perfection, aloof, delay decisions, want more data, "fussy about minor issues"
  • Examples:Colin Powell, Mr. Spock, Joe Friday, Bill Gates, Jackie Kennedy Onassis
  • When communicating with them: Want others to be relaxed, agreeable, and cooperative, and to show appreciation
  • Analysis paralysis
  • Typical Programmer Personality

Three Career Powers

  • Expertise Power
  • Role Power
  • Relationship Power
  • Any idea on which power is rules them all?
  • All powers are not created equal

Role Power

The Least Effective Power

  • Decreases in effectiveness the more it is used
  • Achieve results by compliance
  • May not believe in what they're doing
  • Managers have more role power than individual contributors
  • Manager's role power controls your addiction to food, clothing and shelter.
  • Individual contributors also have role power based on expertise when doing work for peers and work output.
  • You will get compliance not buy in and the outcome will be sub-optimal

Expertise Power

The Second Most Effective Power

  • Increases in effectiveness the more it is used
  • Great if you want to stay in your role
  • You will need to continuously learn new skills advance in your career to stay relevant
  • Individual contributors #2 power is expertise power.
  • Great if you want to stay in your role
  • You will need to continuously learn new skills advance in your career to stay relevant
  • To advance in pay eventually you will need to make a choice management or Architect
  • Another version is subject matter expertise power. (warehouse of bananas)

Relationship Power

The Most Effective Power

  • Increases in effectiveness the more it is used
  • Build trust and good will by helping others
  • In return when you need help they will be there for you
  • Managers and Executives, and Individual contributors all use relationship power to get things done.
  • This is the be biggest lever in your career and has the biggest impact to your career
  • Build goodwill by helping people in your network, both inside your company and outside your company.
  • Give more to your network than you take from your network to build goodwill and use strategically
  • Your relationships with others will help you complete work more efficiently.
  • Your relationships with others help you get heads up on upcoming changes.
  • Producing results on deliverables to others helps build relationships
  • If you have two work items of equal priority strategically work on the one that help build your network of relationships
